Introduction. Our paper presents a neural network trained to estimate the magnetic field of the sun. It learns to predict the magnetic field using recordings taken from the Helioseismic and Magnetic Imager (HMI) onboard the Solar Dynamics Observatory (SDO). This HMI sensor measures polarized light (consisting of I, Q, U, and V measurements across 6 bandpasses), and previously a system called the Very Fast Inversion of the Stokes Vector (VFISV ) would iteratively run a Milne-Eddington solar atmosphere inversion algorithm until it found a set of magnetic field settings that closely matches the real measurements. We emulate this iterative process with a forward model that maps directly from IQUVs to what the magnetic field is. Estimating the magnetic field in this context consists of accurately predicting these eight targets.

  • Field -- The strength of the magnetic field, measured in Mx/cm^2.
  • Inclination -- The inclination angle of the magnetic field, measured in degrees.
  • Azimuth -- The azimuth angle of the magnetic field, measured in degrees.
  • LOS Velocity -- The magnitude of the velocity of the plasma in the line-of-sight of the telescope, measured in cm/s.
  • Doppler Width -- A thermodynamic parameter, measured in milli angstroms.
  • Eta_0 -- A thermodynamic parameter, dimensionless.
  • S0 -- A thermodynamic parameter, measured in DN/s.
  • S1 -- A thermodynamic parameter, measured in DN/s.

Applications. We hope to use this system both as a fast "quick-look" version of VFISV and as a tool for troubleshooting some of the abberations seen in VFISV's output. For example, there appears to be a strange 24-hour periodic tendency in the sun's magnetic field strength.. However this is very suspicious as the sun probably doesn't have 24-hour periodicity, but our satellite observing it definitely has!
