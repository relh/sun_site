Fast and Accurate Emulation of the SDO/HMI Stokes Inversion with Uncertainty Quantification


Introduction. Our paper presents a neural network trained to estimate the magnetic field of the sun. It learns to predict the magnetic field using polarized light measurements (I, Q, U, and V) captured by the Helioseismic and Magnetic Imager (HMI) onboard the Solar Dynamics Observatory (SDO). Our system is trained to emulate an existing system, the Very Fast Inversion of the Stokes Vector (VFISV), that iteratively runs a Milne-Eddington (ME) solar atmosphere inversion algorithm until it finds a set of magnetic field settings that closely match real measurements. Our model leverages previous inversions done in this manner by directly mapping a forward model from IQUVs to magnetic field outputs. Estimating the magnetic field in this context consists of accurately predicting eight targets. Look at the four targets and predictions below and see if you can tell the difference.

Meet the Targets. Now that you've seen some examples, lets talk about what these parameters are. They're strutting their stuff below if you click the play button or move the slider.

  • Field -- the strength of the magnetic field, measured in Mx/cm^2.
  • Inclination -- the inclination angle of the magnetic field, measured in degrees.
  • Azimuth -- the azimuth angle of the magnetic field, measured in degrees.
  • LOS Velocity -- the magnitude of the velocity of the plasma in the line-of-sight of the telescope, measured in cm/s.
  • Doppler Width -- a thermodynamic parameter, measured in milli angstroms.
  • Eta_0 -- a thermodynamic parameter, dimensionless.
  • S0 -- a thermodynamic parameter, measured in DN/s.
  • S1 -- a thermodynamic parameter, measured in DN/s.

Quantitative Results. Although the above comparisons give one a good understanding of qualitative performance, we need to evaluate overall performance across various regions of interest on the solar disk.

As such, we create masks for every image that represent all on-disk pixels (Disk), plage pixels (Plage), active region pixels (AR), and pixels with at least 100 Mx/cm^2 in the absolute value line of sight (100G+).

Evaluation is conducted using the Mean Absolute Error (MAE) and percent of pixels within t. Values for t are target specific and are generated by scaling according to the relative variances. We find that the model reproduces the VFISV output with a high degree of fidelity.

Conclusions. In summary, we have presented in this paper a deep-learning approach for fast and accurate emulation of the HMI pipeline Stokes inversion module. While our approach provides a more efficient way to produce existing information and does not produce new scientific models, it provides a first step towards advances like correcting hemispheric bias in HMI data, removing oscillation artifacts in HMI magnetograms, and extending solar magnetic field measurements with other observation modalities. In these cases, the prospect of correcting errors or making predictions without a corresponding detailed physical model has the potential to dramatically enhance a mission’s scientific value for solar and space research. Seen from this viewpoint, our ability to rapidly emulate the current pipeline is only a beginning.
